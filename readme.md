## 📘 Part A — Theory & Definitions

### 1️⃣ Types of Data

#### 🔢 Numerical (Quantitative) Data

> Data that represents measurable, countable quantities on which mathematical operations are meaningful.

| ✅ Property | 📝 Explanation |
|------------|--------------|
| **Measurable** | Expressed as actual numbers (e.g., income = ₹45,000/month) |
| **Mathematically Operable** | Can compute sum, average, standard deviation, variance |
| **Two Subtypes** | *Discrete* (counts, e.g., Family Size) and *Continuous* (measurements, e.g., Income) |
| **Examples in this dataset** | Age of Household Head · Household Income · Family Size |

<br/>

#### 🏷️ Categorical (Qualitative) Data

> Data that represents labels, groups, or categories rather than quantities — describes qualities.

| ✅ Property | 📝 Explanation |
|------------|--------------|
| **Label-Based** | Values are names or categories, not numeric quantities |
| **Two Subtypes** | *Nominal* (no natural order) and *Ordinal* (ordered, e.g., Education Level) |
| **Valid Operations** | Frequency counts, proportions, mode, bar charts, pie charts |
| **Examples in this dataset** | Education Level · Owns House · Urban/Rural |

---

### 2️⃣ Types of Statistics

#### 📈 Descriptive Statistics

> Summarizes and describes the **features of a known dataset** without drawing broader inferences.

| 📌 Aspect | 📝 Detail |
|---------|---------|
| **Purpose** | Organize, summarize, and present data clearly |
| **Key Measures** | Mean, Median, Mode, Range, Variance, Standard Deviation |
| **Key Tools** | Frequency tables, histograms, boxplots, KDE plots |
| **Example** | *"The average household income is ₹38,250/month"* |
| **Limitation** | Describes only the sample — cannot generalize to a population |

<br/>

#### 🔮 Inferential Statistics

> Draws **broader conclusions about a population** based on a sample, using probability theory.

| 📌 Aspect | 📝 Detail |
|---------|---------|
| **Purpose** | Make predictions and test hypotheses beyond the dataset |
| **Key Methods** | Hypothesis testing, confidence intervals, regression analysis |
| **Key Tools** | T-tests, ANOVA, Chi-Square, p-values |
| **Example** | *"Urban households earn significantly more than rural ones (p < 0.05)"* |
| **Strength** | Enables data-driven decisions that generalize to the real world |

---

## 📐 Measures of Central Tendency

> These measures locate the **center** of a distribution — answering *"What is the typical value?"*

| 📏 Measure | 📝 Meaning | 🎯 Best Used When | ⚠️ Limitation |
|-----------|----------|----------------|-------------|
| **〜 Mean** | Arithmetic average of all values | Data is symmetric, no extreme outliers | Heavily influenced by very high or very low outliers |
| **⬛ Median** | Middle value when data is sorted | Data is **skewed** (like income data) ✅ | Ignores the actual magnitude of extreme values |
| **🔁 Mode** | Most frequently occurring value | Categorical or discrete data | May not be unique — can have multiple modes |

<br/>

### 💡 Key Insight for Income Data

| Relationship | Interpretation |
|-------------|---------------|
| **Mean > Median** | Distribution is **Positively Skewed** → right tail pulled by high earners |
| **Mean < Median** | Distribution is **Negatively Skewed** → left tail pulled by low earners |
| **Mean ≈ Median** | Distribution is approximately **Symmetric** → rare in income data |

> 📌 **For household income analysis**, the **Median is always preferred** over the Mean. A small group of very high-income households can inflate the mean significantly, misrepresenting what a typical household actually earns.

---

## 📏 Measures of Dispersion

> These measures describe **how spread out** the data is — answering *"How variable are the values?"*

| 📏 Measure | 📝 Meaning | 🎯 Best Use Case | 💡 Insight |
|-----------|----------|----------------|---------|
| **↔️ Range** | Maximum − Minimum | Quick rough estimate of total spread | Simple but very sensitive to outliers |
| **📐 Variance** | Average of squared deviations from the mean | Foundation for standard deviation | Expressed in squared units — harder to interpret directly |
| **📉 Standard Deviation (σ)** | Square root of Variance | Comparing spread across groups | Same unit as original data — easy to interpret |
| **📦 IQR** | Q3 − Q1 — spread of the middle 50% | **Outlier detection** — robust to extreme values ✅ | Not affected by very high or very low income values |
| **📊 Coefficient of Variation (CV)** | Standard Deviation ÷ Mean × 100 | Comparing variability across groups | Expressed as a percentage — scale-free comparison |

<br/>

### 💡 IQR & Outlier Detection Rule

| Boundary | Formula | Meaning |
|---------|---------|---------|
| **Lower Fence** | Q1 − 1.5 × IQR | Any income below this → potential low-end outlier |
| **Upper Fence** | Q3 + 1.5 × IQR | Any income above this → potential high-end outlier |
| **High IQR Value** | Large Q3 − Q1 gap | Indicates **wide income inequality** within the dataset |

## 📊 Distribution & Statistical Concepts

### 🔔 Gaussian (Normal) Distribution

> The classic **bell curve** — the foundation of most statistical inference methods.

| ✅ Property | 📝 Detail |
|-----------|---------|
| **Shape** | Perfectly symmetrical, bell-shaped curve |
| **Center** | Mean = Median = Mode — all three equal at the peak |
| **Defined By** | μ (mean) controls location · σ (standard deviation) controls width |
| **Tails** | Extend infinitely but approach zero asymptotically |
| **Real-World Use** | Test scores, heights, measurement errors typically follow this pattern |

**Formula:**

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}$$

---

### 📉 Log-Normal Distribution

> When the **logarithm of a variable** is normally distributed — the standard model for income data.

| ✅ Property | 📝 Detail |
|-----------|---------|
| **Shape** | Right-skewed (positively skewed) — long upper tail |
| **Why Income Follows This** | Income cannot be negative; income growth is multiplicative, not additive |
| **Key Transformation** | Applying log(Income) produces an approximately Normal distribution |
| **Practical Advantage** | Models real-world income inequality and wealth accumulation realistically |
| **Used By** | Economists, central banks, and government agencies globally |

---

### 📐 The 3-Sigma Rule (Empirical Rule)

> For any **normally distributed** variable, the spread follows this reliable pattern:

| 📏 Range | 📊 Data Coverage | 🎯 Practical Meaning |
|--------|---------------|-------------------|
| **μ ± 1σ** | 68.27% of all data | Most income values fall in this central band |
| **μ ± 2σ** | 95.45% of all data | Almost all typical households are captured here |
| **μ ± 3σ** | 99.73% of all data | Virtually the entire population is included |
| **Beyond ±3σ** | Only 0.27% of data | Statistical extreme outliers — exceptionally rare |

> 📌 Any household income beyond μ ± 3σ is a **statistical extreme** — occurring in fewer than 3 out of every 1,000 records.

---

### 📊 Percentiles & Quartiles

| 📊 Concept | 📝 Definition | 🔢 Income Example |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Percentile** | Value below which a given percentage of data falls | P90 = ₹75,000 → 90% of households earn below this |
| **Q1 — First Quartile** | 25th percentile — bottom 25% boundary | Lower-income group threshold |
| **Q2 — Second Quartile** | 50th percentile = Median | Typical household income midpoint |
| **Q3 — Third Quartile** | 75th percentile — top 25% boundary | Upper-middle income threshold |
| **IQR** | Q3 − Q1 — spread of the middle 50% | Core measure of income inequality |

---

### 📦 Five-Number Summary

> A compact, robust description of any numerical variable's full distribution:

| 📌 Position | 📏 Metric | 📝 What It Tells You |
|-----------|---------|-------------------|
| 1️⃣ | **Minimum** | Lowest recorded income — the absolute floor of the dataset |
| 2️⃣ | **Q1 (25th Percentile)** | Income below which the bottom 25% of households fall |
| 3️⃣ | **Median (Q2)** | The midpoint — best single representation of typical household income |
| 4️⃣ | **Q3 (75th Percentile)** | Income below which 75% of all households fall |
| 5️⃣ | **Maximum** | Highest recorded income — the absolute ceiling of the dataset |

---

### ↗️ Skewness

> Measures the **asymmetry** of a distribution — how lopsided the data is around its center.

| 📊 Skew Type | 🔢 Skewness Value | 🔺 Shape | 📝 Interpretation | 🎯 Income Context |
|------------|----------------|--------|----------------|-----------------|
| **Positive Skew** | Greater than 0 | Tail extends to the RIGHT | Most incomes are low; a few are very high | ✅ **Typical for income datasets** |
| **Symmetric** | Approximately 0 | Balanced bell curve | Values evenly spread around the mean | Rare in real income data |
| **Negative Skew** | Less than 0 | Tail extends to the LEFT | Most values are high; a few are very low | Uncommon in income distributions |

> 📌 **Rule of Thumb:** If Skewness > 1.0, the distribution is **strongly right-skewed** — always use **Median** as the preferred central measure, not Mean.

---

### 🧮 Kurtosis

> Measures the **tail heaviness** (peakedness) of a distribution — how extreme the outliers are.

| 🧮 Type | Kurtosis Value | 📝 Shape | 🎯 Practical Meaning |
|--------|--------------|--------|-------------------|
| **Mesokurtic** | ≈ 3 (excess ≈ 0) | Normal bell curve baseline | Standard tail behavior — typical distribution |
| **Leptokurtic** | Greater than 3 | Tall & narrow peak, **fat tails** | More extreme outliers than normal — high-income extremes present |
| **Platykurtic** | Less than 3 | Flat & wide peak, thin tails | Fewer outliers — values cluster close to the mean |

> 📌 **For income data:** Leptokurtic distributions are most common — capturing the heavy upper tail where a small group of households earns vastly more than the median.

### 2️⃣ Central Tendency Analysis

**Variables Analyzed:** Household Income · Age of Household Head

| 📏 Measure | 💰 Household Income | 👤 Age of Head |
|-----------|-------------------|--------------|
| **Mean** | Overall average income level across all households | Average age of all household heads |
| **Median** | Typical income — unaffected by extreme earners | Typical age — robust to age outliers |
| **Mode** | Most common income bracket in the dataset | Most frequently occurring age |

**📝 Key Interpretations:**

- If **Mean > Median** → Income is **positively skewed** — Median should be used for reporting
- The **Mode income** reveals which income group has the highest concentration of households
- A large **gap between Mean and Median** signals significant inequality present in the dataset
- For **Age**, Mean and Median are likely close — age is more symmetrically distributed than income

---

### 3️⃣ Measures of Dispersion

**Variables Analyzed:** Household Income · Age · Family Size

| 📏 Measure | 🎯 What It Reveals for Income Data |
|-----------|----------------------------------|
| **Range** | Total income span from the lowest to the highest earner |
| **Variance** | Overall degree of income spread — higher value = more inequality |
| **Standard Deviation** | Average income deviation from the mean in original currency units |
| **IQR (Q3 − Q1)** | Spread of middle-class incomes — primary inequality indicator |
| **Coefficient of Variation** | Relative variability — enables fair comparisons across income groups |

**📝 Key Insights:**

- **High IQR** → Significant income gap between the middle-class and upper-income groups
- **High CV (above 50%)** → Income is highly variable and unequally distributed
- **Outlier bounds** reveal which households have abnormally high or low incomes
- **Standard deviation** compared to the mean indicates how "spread out" typical incomes are

### 4️⃣ Distribution Analysis

**Target Variable:** Household Income

| 📊 Analysis Step | 🎯 Purpose | 💡 Expected Finding |
|----------------|----------|-------------------|
| **Histogram Plot** | Visualize income frequency across equal-width bins | Right-skewed shape with a long upper tail |
| **KDE Overlay** | Smooth density curve placed over the histogram | Confirms skew direction and peak location |
| **Gaussian Curve Fit** | Compare income shape to an ideal Normal distribution | Income will visibly deviate from normality |
| **Log Transformation** | Apply log(Income) to reduce skewness | Log-Income approximates a Normal distribution |
| **Skewness Calculation** | Quantify directional asymmetry numerically | Expected positive value (greater than 0) |
| **Kurtosis Calculation** | Measure tail heaviness numerically | Expected leptokurtic result (greater than 3) |
